Climbing Mountains

Completed first ever trek - was a truly enriching experience - the entire act of setting out to do something that is at the edge of what’s capable for me, and pushing though all inhibitions to get it done - in an environment cut off from networks and connectivity - meeting folks on the way - connection in a bubble, enroute a common goal - the metaphorical aspect - setting out to achieve a surmounting goal - moving ahead one step at a time - taking breaks and getting to checkpoints - keeping up pace, rhythm and spirit - the act of reaching a peak and that feeling of insignificance of the peak - setting out to the chase the the next nearest peak, it’s never enough - speaks about the pursuit of fulfillment

I just got done with my first-ever trek up Triund, overlooking the Dhauladhar range, starting from Bhagsu, Himachal, to setting up base camp at the top. It was a unique experience that surpassed all expectations. I just got back, descended the trail, explored waterfalls, traveled back to college, and here I am sitting, trying to pen down everything that emerged from within regarding the entire thing. I intend not to make this a journalistic account, but it was a deeply impactful experience. I was surprised by just how many parallels I could find between physically climbing a mountain (undertaking hard, uncomfortable action) and the toils of doing something tough in life.

A lot lies in the act of setting out to do something difficult, something that sits right at the edge of what I deem possible for myself, and then making n number of things line up well enough for it all to add up to reconciliation. It feels rather fulfilling. There was ample reason for me to not do this. Despite the risk involved, my own strength and capacity, and all the toil and bother that came my way, it was an internally rewarding experience to have done it against the odds. For an honest confession, I will admit: there was a sizeable amount of ego involved. Once I set out, not doing this was simply not an option. Not going all the way, or turning back to return to a cozy environment, didn’t even enter my mind. Which is strange; seldom does one get to have that experience.

In today’s day and age, when we live comfortable lives, we are almost never put in situations where giving up is not an option. We live distracted, comfortable lives, and at the first sign of discomfort, we scurry back to our safe spaces, both mentally and physically. One might decide to break that pattern by pursuing intense, challenging activities like running, hitting the gym, or even doing something mentally challenging. But you can always choose to stop. You can stop running to catch a breath, lower your reps at the gym, or get back to distractions. The lack of that "exit" was the most prominent characteristic of this activity.

There is the cliché statement about how the lack of mobile network and connectivity from the rest of the world adds a unique dimension to every experience. However, this was too short a period to emphasize this, barely 15-16 hours. I look forward to grabbing a "digital quarantine" opportunity in the future.

We meet numerous people through our days, but everybody is on their own unique journey. Such was not the case here. We were a group of five, accompanied by two other groups of four, with nobody distracted by the menial toils of the day. Every interaction felt peaceful, calm, and serene. This does sound insignificant in retrospect, but it is a major missing factor in day-to-day routines where everybody is bombarded with distractions and deadlines. It made me ponder about how the most meaningful, deep connections are built when you pursue a goal with utmost dedication; you meet like-minded people. There is much talk these days about loneliness and disconnectedness; this felt like an antidote.

Now to describe the core of the entire exercise: the trek itself. The act of setting out to do something tough and uncomfortable, with little to no certainty about how well the body or mind is equipped. I admit this feels like an overdramatization for an easy trek by most standards, but I’ll describe how I felt about it. To start with, there was an excitement about the experience to come. I’ve been doing some "endurance" training and this felt like a suitable test for that. Ego was a major driving force for the subconscious, such that giving up or settling for "far enough" was never on the table. There was only one acceptable outcome, and my mental faculties were dedicated to everything that would take me there.

It makes one wonder: we often go about making safe, calculated moves, and in the process, we often compromise what we initially set out for. (This parallel is a nuanced argument, so I’ll leave it at that). The seemingly towering task was marked by checkpoints. At any given point, all that mattered was getting to the next one. That helped avoid unnecessary long breaks, stopping not when tired, but only when required for the optimal path to the top.

It also made me appreciate the importance of momentum. It was so much easier to cover distance when I just blocked everything else out and pushed through, sprinting my way across. That was counter-intuitive to a certain degree: how could moving faster be more effective when my legs were so tired? Pace, rhythm, and spirit were key, which prompts one to look at their own roadblocks through this lens.

After we reached the base camp area, there was about an hour-long hike to the snow-covered top. The trek guide stayed back and we were on our own. It was completely up to us how long and how fast we wanted to move. We looked ahead and marked a peak; we dragged ourselves there, and once I stood there, that little piece of achievement felt insignificant. All that mattered was the next peak in sight. It felt like fulfillment is an illusion we chase to keep moving, almost like a subconscious evolutionary trait.

One solid conclusion I could draw for myself personally is how doing something hard (not so hard that it’s impossible, not so easy that it's a cakewalk, but the "right amount" of hard) drives me more than anything.

This is the end of my first blog here on Tiny Torque. This entire blog thing is its own endeavor: putting my word out. To present myself for judgment is definitely not a comfortable effort. But this is what I choose to do. I could be doing numerous different things, but this is something I wanted to get going. This piece of writing was rather haphazardly put together, and it’s almost certain this will seem cringe or odd in retrospect, but that’s fine. From the next blog onwards, I’ll try to focus on more interesting project-related content.
